UWC Atlantic College expectations:
Why you are here!

Learning through residential life

At UWC Atlantic College you will live in a dormitory with 3 other students from different countries to you. They may have different routines and there may be aspects of residential life that you are unfamiliar with.

You will be expected individually and with other members of the community to live by the values upon which the College is founded, respect, responsibility and tolerance.

Thinking about how you interact with others and taking responsibility within the community to contribute joyfully, respectfully and kindly.

Just like at home, there are tasks that need to be completed by everyone in the house; duty dorm for example where you work with your dorm mates as a team to clean the dayroom and kitchen of your student house. You are expected to participate fully in these tasks as then we take ownership collectively of the spaces we occupy together.

Learning to care for yourself and others

Being conscious of your wellbeing and of the community around you can be challenging. For many of you, this is the first time you will have lived away from home and being totally responsible for yourself can sometimes be difficult.

You will need to ensure you get enough sleep, regular physical exercise, eat well, take time for reflection and to recognise the signs in yourself of when you are not doing these. Without taking care of yourself, looking out for your friends and meeting the challenges of a UWC programme becomes difficult.

Intercultural and Interpersonal Awareness

Learning more about other people is one of the main reasons you chose to come to a UWC and one of the most important ways for you to maintain and build the community is by listening carefully to others. Empathy can be learnt and being able to see situations from someone else’s perspective is a skill that will enable you to live your life by the values and mission of the UWC movement.

Want to organise an event?

You are actively encouraged as a student of AC to take initiative and create an event that can add to the patchwork of diversity that is AC. It can be a musical event, a mini conference, a debate, whatever you would like to bring to the community to enjoy and learn about.

It can be hard work to do this but very rewarding and the learning that you will gain from doing it will be very beneficial for your future! It is a responsibility that must be discussed with the Vice Principal, Pastoral who will work with you on the date of the event and the logistics – good luck!

A typical AC Day

Breakfast is taken in the dining hall from 0715 – 0830 (if you have a free code!) lessons start at 08:00. Classes run until lunch time and then there are meetings with your tutor, activities or service sessions. Dinner is served from 18:00 – 19:00 and in the evening you will have personal study time or student led activities, fitness and community events. Check in at your house is at 22:15 and you are expected to be quiet after this in the houses so those who want to rest can do. You cannot leave the house until 0600 as the house is then locked for safety.

Student Support

There are many ways of accessing support at AC; your personal choices like your friends and family, your dorm mates and cultural groups and more structured ways like your houseparent, the resident assistants, the school counsellor and the wellbeing programme.

Your new friends

The people you share your dorm with, your buddy dorm, your fellow students in your house, your cultural group, your friends.

Peer Listeners

The Peer Listeners are Second Year students who have had specific training in listening skills. They are particularly trustworthy, non-judgemental, sympathetic and tactful. Staff do, however, feel that issues of an extremely serious nature (such as rape, physical violence, drug abuse, pregnancy, eating disorders, suicidal thoughts or significant self-harm) are not appropriate to be dealt with by Peer Listeners. If Peer Listeners do encounter such cases, we have asked them to pass them on to a Houseparent, the Counsellor or a Nurse. With the exception of such cases, Peer Listeners will always maintain confidentiality.

Wellbeing @ AC Group

The wellbeing reps provide an informal platform for running wellbeing events at AC. They also work to raise awareness about mental health and organise events, open spaces, etc. for students to explore these issues. Although, there are scheduled events, the group is always open for anyone to attend. You can join in the events, talks, open spaces or find them in your house for any questions, suggestions, opinions and conversations.

Houseparent / Assistant Houseparent

Your houseparent lives in house next to the student house with their family and will become a very important part of your AC experience. You will have individual meetings with them throughout the year and also plenty of occasions when you gather together for house events. They are all trained to guide you through your experience and are available during the evenings when you are not in school. They will also make sure the house is run efficiently, any discipline issues are dealt with and to listen; many of them have counselling skills and all are experienced in the care of young people living away from home for the first time.

Tutors

You are all allocated a Personal Tutor and their main responsibility is to establish a relationship with you which will create an atmosphere of trust in which you feel free to approach them should you have problems, of an academic or general nature. The need for confidentiality is thus essential. They can live on or off site and there are regular times in the week when you will see them and you can also contact them via email to arrange times to see them individually.

Night staff

There are usually two Night Staff who work from 22:00 through the night until 05:30. They provide a point of contact during the night for students who are distressed or unwell. They will also make you go to bed and make sure the campus is safe and secure. They are also trained to listen to you if you have any issues. You can contact them by calling 2222.

Your Health

Physical and mental health

Are equally important and at Atlantic College, there are many ways to look after both. The Moondance Hall has a large activity hall, a dance studio and a gym. After an induction, you are able to use these facilities and also to participate in the activities and classes held there. Please check your house noticeboards for detailed information.

Our Health Centre, based in Kemps Covert, has two full time nurses, a Health assistant and a school counsellor. In the UK, all physical and mental health issues are in the first instance discussed with a GP (general practitioner doctor) who comes to the college twice a week. If they feel that you need to see a specialist, they will refer you to a consultant in whatever area you need.

The National Health Service provides this to all residents of the UK free of charge so sometimes there is a waiting list for some services. If there is an emergency you will be taken to the Accident and Emergency department of the hospital in the nearest town (Bridgend).

Counselling

In the UK, talking therapy is the recommended route in the first instance for a mental health issue. If the GP doctor and the counsellor feel that you need further support, you will be referred to the Children and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) where you will see a psychiatrist and perhaps offered other therapies and / or medication. Very few students have to be referred and the support that they need can usually be found within the college structures already in place.

Jane, our counsellor is very accessible and offers the following:

  • Counselling – a 90 minute talking therapy session followed up by 4 x 50 minute sessions if necessary
  • Wellbeing Check in – a 45 minute session that looks at your lifestyle choices and helps you to make some changes. This can also be followed up by further sessions.

The health centre has a Wellbeing Drop In session every afternoon seven days a week where you can spend 10 – 15 minutes talking to a member of the Health Centre Team who can signpost you to more support and make practical suggestions of how you can help yourself.

EDW, or Excessive Display of Wealth

You will hear this expression at AC a lot! Socio economic diversity is one of our many aspects of diversity at AC and some students come from families that are financially wealthy and others have limited or no financial resources. We know extremes exist but we wish to minimise their impact on student life. Kurt Hahn said “living with reasonable self denial” and as there is plenty of inequality in the world, we do not wish to emphasise it at AC. We ask you to not act in such a way that could be seen as EDW and to live simply and frugally.

At some point in your life, these budgeting skills will prove very useful!

Things to Remember…
  • The sea: if the tide is high or the waves are strong, stay far away from the slipway. People die every year in this area due to the tide.
  • Rocks and pieces of the cliffs periodically fall.
  • If you’re on the beach, stay far away from the cliff edges so you aren’t crushed. If you’re walking along the coastal path, stay far from the edge so it doesn’t crumble under you.
  • The watchtower is out of bounds because it is an unstable structure.
  • Please do not litter in general: this includes food waste, cigarette butts, wrappers, papers, etc…
  • The flagstones outside the castle and the arts center are extremely slippery when wet. Don’t fall on your butt!

The most important thing to remember is that your second years are ALWAYS here for you. It doesn’t matter if we’re in the same house or service or national group, whether we talk every day or not at all – we’re all UWC students here, and in the end we stick together. So if you need something, just ask. J
